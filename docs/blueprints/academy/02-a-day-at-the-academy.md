# A Day at the Academy

> **Purpose of this document**
>
> This document is an observation of how an academy operates during an
> ordinary working day. It intentionally avoids describing software
> solutions. Its goal is to understand the business before designing
> the product.

> **A note on roles**
>
> This document describes roles separately for clarity. In practice,
> especially at smaller institutes, one person may perform multiple
> roles. The receptionist may also be the accountant. The owner may
> also be the counsellor. The workflows remain the same — only the
> number of people involved changes.

---

## Scene 1 — Academy opens

Tuesday, 8:00 AM.

The shutters roll up.

The first person to arrive unlocks the office. Before a single student
walks in, yesterday's problems are still waiting.

Three parents promised to pay yesterday. None of them did.

One faculty member called in sick last night. No replacement was arranged.

A batch that started with40 students now has44 — two more were enrolled
than there are chairs.

The AC repair person was supposed to come yesterday. He didn't. Room 3
is still broken.

The owner sits down with a cup of tea and a handwritten register.
The register shows who has paid, who hasn't, and who is about to default.
It is not up to date.

A staff member walks in. "Ma'am, the AC in Room 3 isn't working."

The day hasn't started yet. There are already five problems.

### What happened?

The owner arrived and immediately faced multiple operational issues:
pending payments, staffing gaps, capacity overflow, incomplete records,
and a maintenance delay.

### What made the work difficult?

The owner spent ten minutes flipping through the register to find
yesterday's numbers. She still isn't sure she has the complete picture.
She doesn't know which parents actually paid versus which ones promised
to pay. The register doesn't distinguish between the two.

### Questions.

- How does the owner currently track pending payments?
- What happens when a faculty member calls in sick? Is there a
  substitute system?
- How is batch capacity tracked today?

## Scene 2 — First enquiry

Tuesday, 9:00 AM.

The phone rings.

"Hello, [Institute Name], how can I help you?"

A parent is calling. Their child is in 11th grade. They want to know
about JEE coaching. What are the timings? What are the fees? Is there
a batch starting soon?

The receptionist answers each question from memory. She has done this
a hundred times. She writes the parent's name and phone number in a
ledger. The column says "Enquiry." Nothing else.

The parent asks: "Do you offer scholarships?"

The receptionist isn't sure. "I'll ask the counsellor and call you
back."

She hangs up. She writes the parent's name on a sticky note and sticks
it on her desk. She will probably forget about this parent by tomorrow.

### What happened?

A potential customer called with basic questions. The receptionist
answered from memory and recorded the enquiry in a ledger with no
follow-up information. One question she couldn't answer required
a callback that may never happen.

### What made the work difficult?

The receptionist couldn't answer a common question without checking
with someone else. The enquiry was recorded with only a name and
phone number — no source, no follow-up date, no indication of
interest level. The sticky note on her desk will be buried by
lunchtime.

### Questions.

- How many enquiries does the institute receive per day?
- What percentage of enquiries convert to admissions?
- How are follow-ups tracked today?
- Does the receptionist know which marketing channel brought the caller?

## Scene 3 — Admission

Tuesday, 11:00 AM.

A parent walks in with their child. They met the counsellor last week.
They've decided to enroll.

The counsellor greets them. They sit down. The counsellor asks for
documents: Aadhaar card, previous marksheet, passport-size photos.

The parent brought photocopies of the marksheet and photos. But the
Aadhaar card is at home. "I forgot. Can we continue? I'll bring it
tomorrow."

The counsellor isn't sure. She asks the owner. The owner says, "It's
fine, but make sure they bring it." The counsellor writes a note on
the admission form: "Aadhaar pending."

The counsellor fills out the rest of the admission form by hand.
Name, parent's name, phone number, address, course, batch. The form
is carbon-papered — one copy for the institute, one for the parent.

The parent asks about fees. The counsellor explains: ₹15,000 for the
full year, payable in3 installments. The first installment is due today.

They walk to the accounts desk. The accountant is on the phone. They
wait5 minutes. The accountant takes the cash, writes a receipt by hand,
and tears it from a receipt book. The parent takes their copy.

The counsellor assigns the student to a batch. She checks a whiteboard
on the wall. "JEE Morning — 38 students." She writes the student's
name in the corner of the board.

But the whiteboard says38, and the owner said44 earlier. The counsellor
isn't sure how many students are actually in the batch. She writes the
name anyway.

The parent and student leave. The admission is complete. It took45
minutes. The Aadhaar is still missing. No one is tracking that.

### What happened?

A parent enrolled their child. Documents were collected (with one
missing), a form was filled, fees were paid, a receipt was written,
and the student was assigned to a batch.

### What made the work difficult?

The missing Aadhaar created a loose end that depends on human memory.
The counsellor couldn't verify batch capacity with confidence — the
whiteboard and the owner's count disagreed. The45-minute process
required the parent to wait while someone else was on the phone.
Three different people touched the same information: counsellor,
accountant, and receptionist.

### Questions.

- What documents are required for admission? Does it vary by course?
- Can fees be paid in installments? How many?
- What happens when a batch is full? Can the owner override capacity?
- Is there a counselling step before admission, or can a student
  enroll directly?
- What happens to documents that are promised but never delivered?

## Scene 4 — Teaching

Tuesday, 2:00 PM.

The afternoon batch begins. Twenty-five students file into the classroom.
The teacher opens the attendance register.

"Rahul? Rahul?"

Rahul is absent. The teacher marks him absent and moves on. No one
knows why Rahul isn't here. No one calls his parent.

Halfway through class, a student raises his hand. "Sir, I was here
yesterday but you marked me absent."

The teacher checks the register. It shows absent. The student insists
he was there. The teacher doesn't remember. There's no way to verify.
He says, "I'll fix it next time." The student is frustrated.

The teacher begins the lesson. A student asks a question about
yesterday's homework. The teacher realizes she forgot to check it.
She writes a note to herself: "Check homework tomorrow."

After class, the teacher reviews test papers from last week. She
marks15 papers. Three students scored below50%. She writes their
names on a sticky note and sticks it on her desk. She will tell the
branch manager tomorrow. Maybe.

### What happened?

A teacher conducted a class, took attendance, identified an absent
student, resolved (poorly) an attendance dispute, and flagged
low-performing students — all using physical notes and memory.

### What made the work difficult?

The attendance dispute had no resolution. Neither the teacher nor the
student could prove who was right. The attendance register is a
one-way record — once marked, there's no audit trail, no way to
correct, no way to verify. The sticky note with struggling students
will sit on the desk until it falls off.

### Questions.

- What happens when a student is absent for3 consecutive days?
- How are test results communicated to parents today?
- Does the teacher track homework completion, or only test scores?
- How does the branch manager know which students are struggling?
- How are attendance disputes resolved?

## Scene 5 — Fees

Tuesday, 4:00 PM.

The accountant sits down with the day's collection. Four parents paid
today. Total: ₹45,000. Three in cash, one via bank transfer.

She opens the ledger. Each student has a row. She writes the amount,
date, and receipt number in each row. She calculates the remaining
balance by hand.

One student's installment was due last week. The accountant flags it.
She calls the parent. No answer. She sends a WhatsApp message: "Dear
Parent, this is a reminder that installment2 of ₹5,000 was due on
[date]. Please pay at your earliest convenience."

The parent replies: "Can I pay next month?"

The accountant doesn't know the policy. She will ask the owner tomorrow.

Meanwhile, the receptionist walks in with a bundle of cash from the
morning collection. She hands it to the accountant. The accountant
counts it. The receipts say ₹12,500. The cash is ₹12,000.

Five hundred rupees short.

The accountant asks the receptionist. The receptionist checks her
records. A parent paid ₹2,000 in cash but didn't take a receipt.
She wrote the parent's name on a piece of paper, but the paper is
gone. They both shrug. The accountant will note it as a discrepancy
and move on.

### What happened?

Fees were collected from multiple parents. The accountant updated
the ledger, followed up on a late payment, reconciled cash received
from the receptionist, and discovered a discrepancy she can't explain.

### What made the work difficult?

The cash discrepancy has no resolution. The missing piece of paper
is the only record, and it's gone. The accountant can't tell the
owner whether the discrepancy is a counting error, a missing receipt,
or something else. The late payment parent is negotiating, and the
accountant has no authority to accept or reject the proposal. She
must wait for the owner.

### Questions.

- What are the payment modes accepted? Cash, UPI, bank transfer?
- Can partial payments be accepted?
- How are discounts or fee waivers approved?
- What happens when a student leaves mid-course? Is there a refund
  policy?
- How does the owner know total outstanding across all students?
- How are cash discrepancies handled?

## Scene 6 — Closing

Tuesday, 6:00 PM.

The last batch leaves. The teachers pack up. The receptionist closes
the front desk.

The owner asks, "How many admissions today?"

The receptionist flips through the admission forms. "Four, I think.
No, wait — one was yesterday. Three today."

The owner asks, "How much did we collect?"

The receptionist checks her notes. "₹45,000. But I'm not sure about
the bank transfer."

The owner asks, "Any absent students?"

The receptionist checks the attendance register. "Two. But one might
have been marked absent by mistake."

The owner writes tomorrow's priorities on a sticky note:
- Call the3 parents who didn't pay yesterday.
- Arrange a substitute for the missing faculty.
- Shift one student from the overfull batch.

She locks the office. She has a rough idea of how the day went. Not
an exact picture. Tomorrow she will ask the same questions again.

### What happened?

The owner tried to review the day's operations but got approximate
answers. The information exists across multiple registers, notes, and
people's memories. No single source of truth.

### What made the work difficult?

The owner spent five minutes asking questions and got answers like
"I think" and "I'm not sure." Every number requires someone to
search through physical records. The owner is making tomorrow's
decisions based on incomplete information.

### Questions.

- How does the owner track whether tomorrow's priorities were completed?
- What metrics does the owner care about most?
- How does the owner compare this week to last week?
- What decisions does the owner make based on today's numbers?

## What we learned

### Business operations we observed

- A parent makes an enquiry.
- A counsellor follows up and schedules a meeting.
- A student is admitted.
- Documents are collected (sometimes incompletely).
- Fees are collected.
- A receipt is issued.
- A student is assigned to a batch.
- Attendance is recorded.
- Absences are noted.
- Attendance disputes arise with no resolution.
- Test papers are marked.
- Struggling students are flagged.
- Payment reminders are sent.
- Cash is reconciled (sometimes with discrepancies).
- The day is reviewed (with approximate information).

### Information people needed

- Student and parent details
- Batch availability and capacity
- Outstanding fees and payment history
- Attendance records
- Attendance corrections and disputes
- Test scores
- Faculty schedule and availability
- Marketing source of enquiries
- Document status (complete or pending)

### Pain we observed

- Information is written in multiple places.
- Parents wait while registers are searched.
- Batch capacity is tracked on a whiteboard that may be wrong.
- Fee tracking depends on handwritten ledgers.
- Follow-ups depend on memory.
- Absent students go un-notified.
- Attendance disputes have no audit trail.
- Low-performing students are flagged on sticky notes.
- Cash discrepancies can't be traced.
- The owner gets approximate answers to important questions.
- Missing documents create loose ends with no follow-up.

---

## Things we should observe in a real academy

Before designing software, we should observe:

- How long admissions actually take.
- How enquiries are followed up over days and weeks.
- How parents negotiate fees and what exceptions are made.
- How faculty substitutions are managed when someone calls in sick.
- What information is searched for most often.
- How disputes are resolved — attendance, fees, batch placement.
- How often registers are corrected and what triggers corrections.
- What the owner actually does with daily numbers.
- How documents are stored and retrieved.
- What happens when a student transfers between batches.
- How the institute handles a student who stops attending without notice.
